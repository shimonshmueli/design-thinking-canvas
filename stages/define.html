Stage 2 · Convergent · Problem space

Define

Sensemaking & gaining insights — converge on a problem worth solving.

Define is the convergent half of the problem-space diamond. The raw material from Discover gets clustered, filtered, and synthesized until the team can state — precisely — what problem it is going to solve, for whom, and what success looks like. The thinking modes flip from exploration to synthesis: reduction, merging, abstraction, sensemaking, categorizing.

The stage ends at the PD marker with a concrete artifact: the Problem Definition (brief). If the brief is vague, everything in the solution space inherits that vagueness. It's also normal for this stage to trigger problem reframing — a loop back into Discover when synthesis reveals the team was chasing the wrong problem.

Comes in

The observation pool from Discover.

Goes out

The Problem Definition (brief): problem statement · users & stakeholders · their needs, wants, aspirations · solution point-of-view (approach) · objectives and constraints · success criteria.

Problem Definition (Brief) — the exit artifact

  • Problem statement
  • Who are the users and stakeholders?
  • What are their needs, wants, aspirations?
  • Solution point-of-view (approach)
  • Objectives and constraints
  • Success criteria

Tools & methods for this stage

Clustering & Affinity Mapping

Grouping raw observations into emergent themes.

Internal Analysis (SWOT, Five Forces)

Framing the problem relative to your organization's position.

Personas

Composite, evidence-based characters that represent distinct user groups — each with goals, behaviors, frustrations, and context drawn from Discover research.

Use Cases & User Scenarios

Narrative descriptions of how a user accomplishes a goal: use cases enumerate the interactions between an actor and a system to achieve an outcome; scenarios tell the richer story around them — context, motivation, interruptions.

Storytelling

Packaging research findings as narrative — a day in the user's life, a journey through the pain point — so the insight lands emotionally, not just intellectually.

Briefing (the Design Brief)

Writing the Problem Definition itself — the document that closes the first diamond.

Value Proposition

A sharp statement of the value the eventual solution must deliver: for whom, what gain it creates or what pain it removes, and why it beats the alternatives.

Requirements Hierarchy

Organizing what the solution must do into a structured tree — from high-level needs down through functional requirements to specific constraints — with priorities attached (e.g., must / should / could).
